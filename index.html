How suspension physics work

Spring (preload / stiffness) — The spring supports your weight. Sag is how much it compresses at rest: target 30% for street ADV. Sag too high = spring too soft. Too low = too stiff or too much preload. Preload only shifts the equilibrium — it does NOT change spring rate or the feel over bumps.

Compression damping — Controls how fast the spring collapses when a wheel hits something. Stiff compression = less travel used per hit, but harsh feeling, small bumps transferred straight to chassis. Soft compression = spring uses full travel, smoother, but risk of bottoming on big hits.

Rebound damping — Controls how fast the spring returns after compression. Too slow (0 clicks out) = packing: each successive bump finds the suspension already partially compressed from the last hit. The bike sinks deeper each cycle. Too fast (30 clicks out) = the wheel kicks back up hard, loses contact with ground, chatters. Correct rebound is the most critical setting for traction.

Natural frequency (fₙ) — How fast the chassis bounces in Hz. ADV bike target: 1.5–2.5 Hz. Higher = stiffer ride, more feedback. Matching front and rear fₙ keeps the bike balanced over bumps.

Damping ratio (ζ) — 0 = no damping (bounces forever). 1 = critically damped (returns to rest without oscillating). Real bikes run 0.25–0.55. Too low = bouncy. Too high = harsh, wheel loses contact.

Washboard resonance — If bumps repeat at the same frequency as your suspension's natural frequency, amplitude grows each cycle. This is why correct rebound matters most on rough roads: if the suspension hasn't returned before the next hit, it packs progressively deeper.

Weight transfer — Braking shifts load forward: front fork dives, rear extends. Acceleration shifts rearward: rear squats, front unloads. Matching suspension stiffness to these forces keeps the bike stable and steering geometry consistent.
